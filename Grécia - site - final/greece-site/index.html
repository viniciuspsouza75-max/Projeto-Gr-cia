Τοποθεσία·Location

A peninsula
reaching into
three seas

  • Southeastern Europe, at the tip of the Balkan Peninsula
  • Borders: Albania (NW), North Macedonia & Bulgaria (N), Turkey (NE)
  • Coastline on the Aegean, Ionian & Mediterranean seas
  • Capital: Athens — named after the goddess Athena
  • Around 6,000 islands and islets; only 227 are inhabited
The flag

Nine stripes for the syllables of “Ελευθερία ή Θάνατος” — “Freedom or Death.” Blue & white echo sea, sky and foam; the cross marks Orthodox Christianity.

Η γη & η θάλασσα·Land & Sea

Geography wrote the menu

80% of Greece is mountainous, the rest is coastline and island — that split shapes almost everything Greeks eat and celebrate.

⛰

Mountains

  • Sheep & goat grazing on rocky slopes
  • Feta and other sheep/goat cheeses
  • Hearty stews, pies, wild greens (horta)
🌊

Coastline

  • 13,676 km of coast — longest in the Mediterranean
  • Fresh fish & seafood as a daily staple
  • Port towns built around fishing and trade
☀

Sun & Soil

  • 250+ sunny days a year
  • Olive trees everywhere — oil is the cooking base
  • Dry hillsides suit grapevines and wine

Γιορτές·Holidays & Festivals

A year, in celebrations

Arranged as they fall across the calendar — each one rooted in either faith or history.

  1. Jan – Mar

    Apokries (Carnival)

    • Three weeks of costumes, parades and street parties before Lent
    • “Tsiknopempti” (Smoky Thursday) — a citywide grilling feast
    • Patras hosts Greece's biggest carnival parade
  2. Mar 25

    Independence Day

    • Marks the 1821 uprising against 400 years of Ottoman rule
    • Coincides with the religious Feast of the Annunciation
    • Military & school parades nationwide
  3. Spring

    Orthodox Easter (Pascha)

    • Greece's most important holiday — date follows the Orthodox calendar
    • Midnight Resurrection service, candles carried home lit
    • Red-dyed eggs cracked to symbolise Christ's rebirth
  4. Oct 28

    Ochi Day

    • “Ochi” = “No” — PM Metaxas's 1940 refusal of Italy's ultimatum
    • Commemorates Greece's entry into WWII
    • Parades across every town and school
  5. Dec 25 – Jan 1

    Christmas & New Year

    • Children sing kalanda carols door to door
    • Coastal towns traditionally decorate boats, not trees
    • Saint Basil (not Santa) brings gifts on Jan 1st
    • Vasilopita cake cut at midnight, with a coin hidden inside

Πολιτισμός·Culture

Worn, played & practiced

Clothing

  • The fustanella — a pleated kilt, still worn by the Evzones presidential guard
  • 400 pleats symbolise 400 years under Ottoman rule
  • Regional costumes vary by island, often richly embroidered

Music & Dance

  • The bouzouki, a long-necked lute, anchors Greek folk music
  • Rebetiko — Greece's own "blues," born from 1920s refugee communities
  • Circle dances like sirtaki and kalamatianos

Customs

  • Filoxenia — hospitality as a near-sacred duty to guests
  • Name days (giortές) often celebrated more than birthdays
  • Komboloi worry beads, clicked out of habit, not religion

Γαστρονομία·Gastronomy

What's on the table

Daily meals
  • Breakfast: coffee, bread, honey, yogurt — light & quick
  • Lunch is the main meal, often eaten 2–3pm
  • Dinner is late, sometimes after 9pm
  • Base ingredients: olive oil, vegetables, legumes, bread, cheese
Street food
  • Souvlaki — grilled meat skewers
  • Gyros — spit-roasted meat in warm pita
  • Koulouri — sesame bread ring, grabbed on the go
  • Spanakopita — spinach & feta pie
National & traditional
  • Moussaka — eggplant, potato, minced meat, béchamel
  • Horiatiki — the classic Greek salad
  • Tzatziki — yogurt, cucumber, garlic dip
  • Fasolada — bean soup, often called the national dish
Holiday dishes
  • Easter: roast lamb, red eggs, tsoureki sweet bread
  • Magiritsa — offal soup breaking the Lent fast
  • Christmas: melomakarona & kourabiedes cookies
  • New Year: vasilopita cake with a hidden coin

and the Aegean below

Ξέρατε ότι...·Fun Facts

Did you know?

Greece has around 6,000 islands and islets — only 227 are inhabited.

Its coastline runs 13,676 km, the longest in the Mediterranean.

Athens is widely considered the birthplace of democracy and Western philosophy.

The Olympic Games began in ancient Olympia around 776 BC; the modern Games were revived in Athens in 1896.

Everyday English words like "democracy," "philosophy" and "mathematics" all come from Greek roots.

No point on the Greek mainland is more than 137 km from the sea.
